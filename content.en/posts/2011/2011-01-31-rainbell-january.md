*Rain Bell*

01-01 00:09 Scattered firecrackers went off around the compound, sounding oddly out of place in all this darkness. Most people must already be asleep. I kissed my wife on the forehead, watched her drift off, then stepped onto the balcony. The red light on the camera mounted to the tripod blinked steadily like the eye of a Terminator. Far below, the white steel helmets of the sentries caught the searchlights in cold flashes, as if to remind me that time means nothing. It is only a ruler with which we measure our own suffering.

01-01 00:50 Yesterday's surveillance yielded quite a lot. At the very least, we now know that the snow-camouflage unit with "Special Biological Epidemic Prevention" on their chest tabs is studying plant immunity. Two captains who move in and out frequently are botany specialists. Their shoulder patch shows a soldier in a helmet holding a stalk of wheat, and their notebook covers are stamped in gold with the name Malianwa University. For days now they have been studying the plant species growing inside wounded humans and animals, as well as the causes of death.

01-01 01:09 Old Ma's son failed to stash the iPhone inside the clubhouse office, so we'll have to think of another way to bug the place. We spent the day in the chatroom discussing escape routes. Everyone's GPS still works, and with offline maps we sketched out two options: one southward, entering Yunnan by the Chengdu-Panzhihua expressway, the other northwest along the Chengdu-Lanzhou route toward Qinghai. I favor the second. The plateau has far less vegetation, so it is obviously less likely to be affected.

01-01 22:39 Today the compound recovered a little of its usual atmosphere. The courtyard was crowded, and everyone was forcing out New Year smiles. The troops brought in some canned food, stacked it into pagoda-like towers, and organized games where residents could win prizes. These were the most grimly serious games I have ever seen in my life. Every contest devolved into life-or-death arguments, because the ownership of every single can had become a question of continued survival.

01-01 22:56 I won eleven cans today: five from a slow-bicycle race and six from a ping-pong wheel game. For the first time ever, my wife praised the money I'd spent on my bicycle and racket as having been worth it. I stayed away from any game involving bodily contact. Almost every one of those ended in shoving matches, and the military police had to point guns at both sides to make them stop. Several of the middle-aged women from property management were competing in shuttlecock kicking. They looked as though they hadn't eaten properly in days, so unsteady on their feet that they dropped to their knees begging the soldiers for a consolation prize.

01-01 23:08 Chengdu has gone completely dark now, soaked through by the freezing rain peculiar to New Year's Day, like a cardboard box hauled out of water. The ground is covered with several centimeters of ashy mud. Every building has lost its former color and been reduced to the same dirty, withered tone. It has been raining on and off since dawn, and outside the window, besides the sound of rain, that low, oppressive groan of twisting steel, like some gigantic beast, has risen over the city once again.

01-01 23:22 Today one of the officers let slip that in a few days they would be withdrawing north. The satellite connection confirmed it. For the first time today, we received an official push notification: Lanzhou had been temporarily elevated into the capital, and the Lanzhou Military Region had established an overall disaster-response command. All personnel and supplies are pulling westward. The government has restored radio service on AM110.1, broadcasting evacuation plans and route maps. There is still no news, only survival manuals and official bulletins.

01-02 08:39 Early this morning Chenglong Road was already filling with cars and pedestrians trying to leave the city, no doubt because they heard yesterday's broadcast. I had no idea there were still so many people in the urban districts. God knows how many are still clinging to life in all those hidden lanes and blocks. The checkpoints are much looser now. The strict immunity inspections of the previous days have been reduced to a few simple questions. Our compound, however, remains stubbornly sealed. But hungry people cannot be locked up for long.

01-02 12:24 It has started sleeting outside, and the temperature inside the apartment has dropped below freezing. My wife stuck warming patches on herself and curled up under the quilt with Mango in her arms. On the computer I exported the photos from the past sixty hours into an animation, hoping to track the sentries' shift changes. Instead I discovered that six body bags were taken out of the compound late last night. No new wounded have been brought in these past two days. So either the bags did not contain corpses, or they contained residents from our compound. What the hell are they doing?

01-02 14:18 At noon I told the others what I had found. Old Ma said we had to get the iPhone into the clubhouse no matter what. In truth there has always been an opportunity to place the bug. The only reason he hesitated was that he couldn't bear sending his own son through the ventilation duct. This time he finally hardened himself. PL quietly asked me whether we had too many people now. Counting the families of the nine of us already in the chatroom, that makes twenty-five people total. Starting eight cars at once would alert every sentry in the compound. I told him the more people we have, the better our odds of staying alive.

01-02 15:04 Someone from property management just came knocking. It was the same men I injured before. They pounded for a long time, clearly intending not to leave until I opened up. My wife had only just gotten up and was still packing. This time we're not packing just emergency survival gear, so naturally there is too much we want to take. Mango is a problem. A cat isn't like a dog that can be carried along at all times, especially not a pampered house cat that's afraid even of mice. Damn it! Someone is smashing the door! 2

01-02 15:41 They've finally run off. The living room is a wreck. The glass tabletop is shattered, and there's a large pool of blood on the floor. Those three guards only wanted to strip us of food and water. I shot through the arms of two of them, and I think the third one may have a broken humerus. My forehead has been split open in a triangular gash, and my right ribs ache dully. Thank God my wife locked the bedroom door in time. I should never have opened the door. I was not prepared enough for cruelty. I will not make that mistake twice.

01-02 17:14 Old Ma's son pulled it off. Good for him! PL has synced the incoming feed to my laptop. The iPhone has been tucked inside the grille over the air-conditioner drain hole, so all we can see are feet moving back and forth inside the room. No one is speaking. There's only the low hum of the instruments. The real experiments are probably happening in a different room. I'm hoping they might still say something here that gives the game away. My wife says Mango is nowhere to be found. Could she have bolted out during the chaos earlier?

2011-01-02 21:07 We've been listening for hours and have roughly pieced it together: our compound is a closed sample set of cases. "Every stage of the condition is represented." "Humans, animals, and plants are all typologically useful." "At the current stage it is no longer infectious to humans." "But not a single one can be let go" ... Put all of that into one sentence and the meaning is simple: we are lab rats. There is only one road left now. I had originally thought we could hold out at home for at least three months.

2011-01-02 22:32 The rain is still falling on and off, and we've decided to leave tonight. Three families have backed out, unwilling to take the risk. That leaves six households, fifteen people, and five vehicles. The exit point will be the fence between Buildings 9 and 10. Its base has already been sawn nearly through with a wire saw, and all we need to do is hook a tow line to it and pull it down with a car. Just now, while loading supplies, I discovered that the rear window of my Robin Hood had been smashed. The drinking water stored in the car is gone. Luckily the vehicle itself is fine, and none of the tools were taken.

2011-01-03 03:51 What just happened was genuinely hair-raising. We set out at exactly three in the morning, but only four vehicles made it out. The Subaru's chassis was too low and got hung up at the last moment on the concrete footing of the fence. They opened fire. PL's Cherokee had a side window shot out, Old Ma's Tiggo took a graze to the front tire, and the plastic sheeting I had used to patch my rear window got ripped away by the wind. Only Rabbit's Range Rover escaped untouched. We're in Sansheng Township now, stopping to sort out the cars.

2011-01-03 04:12 Our route was supposed to head south to the beltway, then swing west onto Dajian Road and keep going toward Ya'an. In the darkness, the skyline ahead was lit red by fire. I covered the rear window with a wooden board. The Tiggo is flimsy, and its spare tire is already on. We now have eleven people total: PL, Rabbit, and I are each with our wives; Old Ma has his family of three; and Blade and his wife, who had no car of their own and had originally split up between different vehicles, are now both riding in the Range Rover.

2011-01-03 05:00 The entire beltway has been dug apart. On the far side stand armed soldiers and tanks. No wonder the troops on the Third Ring had all withdrawn. They were redeploying to fortify the outer ring. Now we have no choice but to turn back and try circling north outside the Third Ring instead. I should have stood my ground earlier. We should have taken the northern route from the beginning. On the way we passed a Shell station, and the others all stopped to fill fuel cans. I tuned the radios in three of the cars. The Tiggo has no radio, so I gave Old Ma a walkie-talkie.

2011-01-03 09:59 Before dawn we spent hours looping slowly from south to east along side roads, because we were still trapped inside Chengdu's hinterland. At the junction of the Chengdu-Chongqing Expressway and the beltway, we hit an outbound checkpoint, but we had no immunity certificates and were denied passage. PL tried to argue, and the sentry simply raised his rifle. We turned around at once. The beltway is sealed like an iron barrel. We've decided to follow it counterclockwise and see whether any gap appears.

2011-01-03 10:16 When we reached the area near Shiling, we could go no farther. The Chengdu-Nanchong Expressway was clogged with abandoned cars, all burned out, and the air was thick with suffocating toxic fumes. We put on our masks, discussed it briefly, and decided to turn left and drive back into the urban districts, hoping to skirt this blocked stretch and continue north. If luck is with us, maybe we can still find an epidemic-control station with staff inside willing to issue us certificates.

2011-01-03 14:17 We wound our way through the city center. Everywhere lay thick ash. Darkened buildings gave off a scorched smell, and curled electrical wires were tangled together with dead trees like a burned-out dark forest. We searched the whole morning without seeing a single military vehicle or epidemic-control point. Ragged people flickered through the streets and alleys, watching us with numb, empty faces. In many places there were clear signs of looting, and some of the gray mounds on the ground were unmistakably corpses.

2011-01-03 14:27 I worked out that we were not far from Jianshe Road, where several large shopping complexes stand. The other families had set out with far less in reserve than I had; you could say they went on the road in haste. I suggested that we restock at Ito Yokado, and everyone agreed. Old Ma noticed a smashed military vehicle by the roadside and warned us to stay alert. After a night of freezing rain and mud, our cars looked empty from a distance. Only Rabbit's orange Range Rover stood out a little too much.

2011-01-03 16:12 The street-facing windows and doors of Ito Yokado on Jianshe Road were all shut tight, but the barrier at the entrance to the underground parking garage had been rammed apart. We hesitated only a moment before filing in. There were plenty of cars in the garage, none of them used for a long time and all coated in black ash. We stopped at the supermarket entrance on basement level one and were stunned to find a Hummer H5 parked there. One look at the plate told us it belonged to the neighbors who had escaped from our compound a few days ago.

2011-01-03 16:35 The H5 looks as though it has been sitting there for two days. The packs on board are still inside, but the people never came back. The supermarket's rolling shutter has been pried open. We turned the Grand Cherokee's high beams on into the darkness beyond the entrance, and Blade, Old Ma, and I put on headlamps and prepared to go in while the others stayed with the vehicles. I took the steel crossbow, Blade a machete, and Old Ma scavenged a fire axe on the spot. There were footprints on the floor from earlier intruders.

2011-01-03 18:13 The supermarket shelves were still remarkably intact. It seems the place had been evacuated before looting ever broke out in earnest. Old Ma and I added water and quick foods like sausages and smoked rabbit to our haul. PL and Rabbit very nearly packed their cars full. I warned them not to overload the suspension, so PL took some of the extra water back out. Rabbit, as usual, paid no attention. His vehicle already had the most passengers, and Blade and his wife also loaded up plenty of supplies. Then I heard a noise deeper inside the garage, somewhere down on level B2.

2011-01-03 19:33 By the time we fled out of Ito Yokado's underground garage, night had already fallen. We never found the neighbors who had come in the Hummer, and of course we didn't run into any zombie siege either. What we did see on level B2 were clusters of plants. They had the same tiny gray triangular leaves as the shoots growing from the eyes of that dead cat back in the compound. What truly frightened me was the base of one root cluster. Around it was looped a wedding ring.

2011-01-03 20:45 The uncanny scene at Ito Yokado is still fixed before my eyes. The others all say they didn't see anything; they only heard me shout "Run!" and rushed out after me. I warned everyone again that they must not touch any plant life at all, not even fallen leaves. We're not getting out of Chengdu tonight, so we need to find somewhere to bed down. Following Jianshe Road up to the First Ring Road, the University of Electronic Science and Technology lies to the north. We might as well camp on campus. After more than ten hours of scrambling, we are still only twenty kilometers from where we started.

2011-01-03 23:37 We parked the cars in a line beneath the basic-teaching building at UESTC. There are no greenbelts visible here, and all that concrete is strangely reassuring. Everyone is huddled inside the vehicles, taking turns on watch. I drew first shift. My wife is lying on the back seat under a thin blanket. I have the walkie-talkie and the steel crossbow, and I'm crouched under a rain sheet in the stone corridor opposite the teaching building. The inner city is not as quiet as the outskirts beyond the Third Ring. Sounds keep rising and falling all around, and a few buildings still show traces of light inside.

2011-01-04 08:21 Blade, who had the last watch, woke me at six this morning. Outside the cars everything was wet and glazed with a thin layer of ice. We boiled milk tea in a camping pot on the steps, while PL gathered a pile of books to burn for warmth. The campus was utterly silent. In the distance, the wooded paths and the sports field were buried under ginkgo leaves mixed with mud. All the trees are dead now, and branches keep dropping at random. Blade and Rabbit said they wanted to take a look around campus. Both of them graduated from UESTC.

2011-01-04 10:55 Blade came back from the campus clinic with some gauze and alcohol. Rabbit had been gone so long that we couldn't even raise him on the radio. Only an hour later did he finally stagger back, still in shock. He had gone to see his old dormitory and been terrified by the sight of a student dead in bed. In his words, a patch of moss had grown across the mattress, and if you scraped back the dirt beneath it, bits of bone were still there. We've just turned east from the Chuanshaan Interchange now. You still can't get out of the city that way.

2011-01-04 15:06 We lingered around the North Third Ring for a long time. It was almost noon before we finally discovered a short stretch north of Longtan Interchange with no sentries on it. We drove straight across and at last got out of the city again. The Tiggo's fender got scorched melting as it crossed a firebreak trench. Let's hope the undercarriage can survive whatever comes next. The Chengdu-Mianyang Expressway is also blocked solid with abandoned vehicles, but if we're lucky, the ordinary road beside it may still let us head north all the way toward Deyang and Mianyang.

2011-01-04 15:09 Beyond the North Third Ring lies Chengdu's largest panda conservation base. By the roadside we saw what looked like the bones of a panda. Not infected, hunted. Xindu and Qingbaijiang are still technically part of Chengdu. This area is one of the city's rural-urban pilot zones, with hardly any farmland left in sight, only silent smokestacks and vast empty factory compounds. The built-up area of Xindu looked abandoned too. We drove past it as carefully as we could.

2011-01-04 21:50 If the GLONASS fix is accurate, we are now somewhere between Qingbaijiang and Shifang, still technically inside Chengdu's administrative bounds. Since noon we have spent the day zigzagging around burning towns, covering 153 kilometers with appalling inefficiency. The GPS signal is gone, but luckily I have an old Russian navigation unit. The main problem is that the entire display is in Russian, so I have to guess at it by matching the screen against the map.

2011-01-04 22:02 The cars are parked out in the middle of open country. No dogs barking, no cooking smoke anywhere. Across a narrow water ditch stands a dead bamboo grove, and whenever the wind passes through, a shower of bamboo leaves rattles down. In a normal year, the faster-growing rapeseed would already be blooming by this season. Here the fields are nothing but blackened earth, as though the crops had all been burned down like straw. Blade fetched water from a nearby farmhouse well so we could wash up, but none of us dares drink it.

2011-01-04 22:48 Wearing electrician's gloves, Blade cut down some bamboo and piled it into a bonfire. Before long it started raining again, so we all retreated to the vehicles. I kept scanning back and forth across the usual radio frequencies and heard nothing at all. At our current rate, the fuel in the cars will last only as far as Mianyang at most. Old Ma suggested that from tomorrow on we should follow the gas-station symbols on the map and log every station we find, whether it still has fuel or not. We need to move faster tomorrow, assuming nothing goes wrong.

2011-01-05 08:22 There was an earthquake before dawn. The flashlight I had propped on the dashboard fell over. By feel alone I'd put it at around magnitude four, which is a basic survival skill for anyone from Sichuan. When first light came, I noticed more than a dozen military trucks parked on another road about a kilometer away. Through the binoculars I could see People's Armed Police plates on them. Someone must have fallen asleep on watch last night, because no one noticed them until now. I woke the others immediately so we could decide whether to hide or go over and make contact.

2011-01-05 09:23 The PAP sentries had actually spotted us long before we woke up. Once they saw us moving, they waved us over. It turned out they were troops from the Chengdu People's Armed Police Command College, relocating toward Mianyang. They had a field kitchen truck, braised pork hocks, electricity, and they treated us to steaming bowls of *ranmian*. I asked whether we could trail their convoy, but one PAP instructor said no. Their route ran entirely through military-control zones. A soldier's duty, he said, is obedience. Please understand.

2011-01-05 22:10 From morning until afternoon, in a cold rain, we kept trailing the PAP convoy from a distance. They had refused us in words, but in practice they tacitly allowed it. That made today's progress much faster than yesterday's. By a little after three we had already skimmed past Deyang. The land out here is just as desolate, but unlike Chengdu, the retreat here seems to have spun out of control. Luggage is strewn everywhere, and discarded shoes lie by the roadside.

2011-01-05 22:39 Somewhere near Luojiang, roughly thirty kilometers from Mianyang, a platoon leader came down from the PAP convoy and told us that from this point on we really could not keep following them. We needed to head east onto the Chengdu-Mianyang Expressway, he said. That was the officially designated evacuation route. Sure enough, before long we saw roadside signs reading No Entry and stone markers engraved with nuclear symbols. I knew then that we were drawing close to the nuclear-industrial zone around Mianyang.

2011-01-05 23:18 This was the first real split in our group. Should we follow the government's evacuation route, or stick to the original plan and head west along the Chengdu-Lanzhou corridor? We put it to a vote, and the minority yielded to the majority. In the end the group chose the safer Baoji-Chengdu line. I was in the minority. Mianyang looks to be in better shape than Chengdu. There are fewer high-rises here, mostly broad factory buildings like container terminals, all roofed in the same gray-blue metal. We stopped for the night in the yard of an empty factory.

2011-01-06 00:14 This is the second night we've spent outdoors. I lashed several bags and spare fuel cans onto the roof, and at last there is enough room in the back of the car to lie flat. My wife and I climbed into our separate sleeping bags and reached out to hold hands. Old Ma has first watch. To avoid another stupid mistake like last night, we've shortened each shift, and every person now has to stand watch twice. This vast factory yard is more frightening than the wilderness. Shadows thicker than the night itself are pressing down around us.

2011-01-06 00:31 I had just started to drift off when a low mechanical rumble woke me again. Old Ma had heard it too. Seeing me jump out of the car, he made a hand signal. I followed the direction he pointed and saw that lights had come on inside a factory building several hundred meters away. The noise was a generator. Neither of us had brought headlamps, so we felt our way over in the dark and peered through the window. It looked like a canned-food processing plant. In one corner were piled stacks of empty green tin cans.

2011-01-06 00:32 There were around twenty people living inside, old and young together, and all the men were wearing dark blue work uniforms. Their bedding was spread out across the floor, with basins and kettles scattered everywhere. Several people were sitting around a long worktable strewn with dead chickens. Efficiently, almost expertly, they were plucking feathers, then using heavy shears to cut branches off the chickens' bodies, if those things could still be called chickens, before tossing them into a gasoline drum filled with boiling water. My stomach lurched so hard I nearly vomited.

2011-01-06 00:40 Old Ma and I crept back to the cars and woke everyone without turning on any lights. I don't know what kinds of people are living in those factory buildings now. I don't know whether someone might throw me into an oil drum in the middle of the night. I would rather starve than eat that kind of thing. We agreed to get everyone back into the vehicles and leave at once. Fortunately the roads in the Mianyang development zone haven't been wrecked yet, so we can still drive fast. Back to the open country. Don't let the shadows cover my eyes.

2011-01-06 09:05 I dreamed that I was rereading a story from *Tihu Notes*. In Hexi there is said to be a shrub whose seeds, once eaten by beasts, cling undigested to the intestinal wall. Only after the animal dies do they sprout where the carcass falls, feeding on the corpse and growing into a fresh thicket. Local herdsmen, in winter, often find frozen animals with branches protruding from their mouths, noses, and bodies. I woke sticky with cold sweat all over me. When I climbed up to look outside, the sky was hanging so low and dark it seemed ready to scrape the earth.

2011-01-06 09:10 We drove more than ten li last night before stopping on a country road black as spilled ink. Ditches run along both sides of the road, and in places it is so narrow you can't even turn around. Thinking about it calmly now, the people in that factory probably weren't desperate enough to be eating mutated poultry themselves. More likely they were hardening their consciences and supplying food to the nuclear plants nearby. Beneath our feet, hundreds or perhaps thousands of meters down, who knows how many tunnels and caverns lie hidden, burying the stories of whole lives.

2011-01-06 11:31 We were driving north along the Chengdu-Mianyang provincial road, and the road conditions were terrible. Every so often there would be shell craters one or two meters wide yawning up in front of us. At the crossroads of a deserted town, we spotted from afar a yellow pickup parked by the roadside with an orange warning beacon on top, the sort normally used by electric-repair crews. Three men were squatting beside it. When they saw our cars, they stood and waved. PL's Grand Cherokee was in front, and when he stopped I had no choice but to brake as well. I did not get out.

2011-01-06 11:31 They seemed to want a ride, and PL refused them. The three men lingered by PL's window trying to wear him down, while one of them slowly edged around toward the driver's side. They looked as though they had weapons tucked away somewhere, and I was just starting to feel that something was very wrong when Rabbit, of all people, climbed out of the Range Rover behind us and lit a cigarette. The three men immediately closed in on the Range Rover. Rabbit tried to get back inside, but a club came down on his head and dropped him instantly. Damn it, they're trying to take the car.

2011-01-06 12:01 For the first time I discovered that Blade lives up to his name. His fighting ability is terrifying. With one thrust of a jungle knife he drove the man who had climbed into the driver's seat straight back out. Old Ma and I both jumped from our vehicles with weapons in hand, and the other two attackers turned and ran. The man who'd been stabbed convulsed a few times and then stopped moving. Blood sprayed all over the dashboard. This should count as self-defense, I told myself, though sadly there is no more 110 for me to call.

2011-01-06 12:01 Rabbit seems to be basically all right. He has a huge lump on his forehead and is sitting on the ground resting. The bigger problem is the man who got knifed. When Old Ma and PL dragged him toward the roadside, they found that his left hand had already calcified, with an extra bony spur sticking out where a little finger might have been. So this was someone who had eaten contaminated plants. Burning the body would have been too wasteful, so we piled stones and weeds over him as best we could and drove on.

2011-01-06 13:50 Rabbit is injured after all, so Blade has taken over driving his car while Rabbit lies in the back seat. Blade drives like a maniac. In a matter of moments he had pulled so far ahead that we lost sight of him entirely. Across the wasted fields, we could see the Chengdu-Mianyang Expressway running parallel to us about a kilometer away, with hundreds of people trudging slowly along it under their luggage. I called the Land Rover over the radio, and Blade answered that they had found supplies and were waiting for us in the town ahead.

2011-01-07 08:25 We spent the third night since departure in a small town. Strictly speaking, though, it wasn't a town at all, but a logistics post that Blade discovered by taking a wrong turn at noon yesterday. Camouflage barracks stand interspersed among gray-brick houses. Since arriving here, our radios and electronic devices have all been subjected to heavy interference, and I've lost the records I'd kept over the past few days. The interference comes in on a pattern, once every two hours. I suspect we're still somewhere above a nuclear base.

2011-01-07 10:34 Last night all I knew was that it had been raining. At dawn I found a skin of ice on the utility poles and tree branches. Quite a few of the few trees along the field ridges had come down. The Tiggo wouldn't start this morning and has been idling to warm up ever since. Several of us, wearing gloves, carefully cleared branches from the road ahead. Rabbit slept through the night. The swelling on his head from yesterday has gone down, but he's somehow started running a low fever. I'm the only one carrying a proper medical kit, so I found him some medicine.

2011-01-07 17:07 We reached Mianyang shortly after lunch today. From a distance it looked just like Chengdu, a lime-gray forest breathing black smoke. We had planned to continue north along the Chengdu-Mianyang Expressway, but at the Qing'an ferry crossing on the Anchang River we were stopped by soldiers and police. These troops, wearing a trefoil insignia I had never seen before, didn't check immunity certificates. Instead they ordered us to proceed to a waiting zone marked out along Mianxing North Road in Mianyang's western suburbs and remain there until transport could be moved onward under unified traffic control.

2011-01-07 22:24 The people maintaining order inside the waiting zone are, for the first time in a long while, actual police officers. There are even a few men in urban-enforcement uniforms wearing armbands that say "Patrol." One police officer with two stars on his shoulders tried to make us leave our cars in a temporary lot and move into the prefab dormitories, but we refused in no uncertain terms. This waiting zone in the western suburbs holds several hundred vehicles and perhaps two or three thousand people. Everywhere I look I see either guarded suspicion or outright ill intent. I've realized that the open wilds are safer after all.

2011-01-07 23:47 We've tried talking with the people around us. Most are from nearby counties and small towns. The longest any of them has already been waiting here is three days. Military trains are running back and forth between Mianyang and Baoji, but only two cars on each train are allowed to carry civilians. The police here are SWAT units transferred up from Yibin. The ordinary police structure has already ceased to function. I also spotted those snow-camouflage biological epidemic-prevention troops again, offering free health checks inside the lines. Almost without thinking, I warned everyone not to take any pills they hand out.

2011-01-08 09:09 At dawn I woke to the sound of something tapping against the glass. A cluster of children stood outside, each holding an empty mineral-water bottle and staring at us through the window with pleading eyes. I was about to look for a half-finished bottle to give them when I caught sight, in the corner of my eye, of several grown men standing farther off. They were obviously using the children to do their asking for them. So I waved no and lay back down. The children left unwillingly. A moment later I heard several harsh metallic scraping sounds, jumped out of the car, and found deep fresh gouges already carved into the door.

2011-01-09 12:51 During the thirty-six hours we've spent in the waiting zone, dozens more vehicles have kept arriving behind us. Among them, unmistakably, was our neighbors' Hummer H5, only now it was being driven by several strangers. In two days I've heard train whistles sound ten times, and fewer than a thousand people have been taken away. A porridge shed has been set up by the river, and many families short on supplies have been queueing there. I went over last night to look. It turned out to be coarse rice gruel boiled with a few leaves thrown in, so I did not dare eat it.

2011-01-09 12:57 Rabbit has now been running a low fever for three days. He has very little energy and stays in a daze most of the time. I suspect tetanus, but Rabbit has no visible wound anywhere. I went to the police on duty to ask for help and was told there were no doctors and no medical station. So we've tucked Rabbit into a sleeping bag, dosed him with antipyretics, and kept him asleep in the car. In the meantime those children begging for water have come back twice more, and Blade chased them off without the slightest hesitation by pulling a knife on them. I told him to keep a lower profile.

2011-01-09 13:29 A policeman finally came over just now to inform us that our three vehicles could go up onto the expressway, but Old Ma's Tiggo was being denied passage. Naturally we refused to accept that and demanded to know why they were splitting up the convoy.
