2011-02-01 00:45 Overnight the government set up porridge kitchens all along the main streets of the county town, serving pilaf and clear noodle soup. Unexpectedly, I even saw blue tents marked with the United Nations emblem. At last, medical vehicles were visible again, and my wife ran off to collect some glucose and cold medicine. It was already after midnight. The smell of roasting mutton was drifting everywhere, and for a moment I felt as though I had slipped back many years into a night market in the northwest. I have a feeling we're going to be staying here for a while.

2011-02-01 00:53 There are more guns on the streets now, and oddly enough they act as a kind of balance. The little town has grown quite peaceful. Everyone has relaxed for the moment, as if this place were the end of the road. Blade found the black market and traded canned fish for a few boxes of ammunition. We've parked on the outskirts, far from the town center, where there used to be potato plots. Many travelers without shelter are camped along the roadside, mostly people who got here by hitching rides after their electric scooters ran out of power. They're too exhausted to move and short on supplies.

2011-02-01 21:37 Today is the first day of February. In another two weeks it will be Spring Festival, and I have no idea what state we'll be in by then. This morning we went to the tents bearing the UN insignia. They're UNDP people, and today they were recruiting UNVs. Our motives, of course, were anything but pure. We only wanted one more layer of protection. Plenty of others had the same idea. When we signed up, military police were handing out application forms. My instincts screamed at me not to fill them in honestly.

2011-02-01 21:44 In the end, only my wife and Old Ma got UNV assignments. My wife is helping distribute drinking water, and Old Ma, along with many other volunteers, has been assigned to group and register the outsiders in Xunhua county who show cold symptoms. UNV work comes with meals: self-heating military rice packs. I was actually selected too, but they wanted me to guard refrigerated trucks filled with samples taken from the disease dead, and I refused.

2011-02-02 13:03 Before dawn this morning, a dense burst of gunfire tore through the little town, like a string of ten thousand firecrackers going off at once. By late morning we learned that several military police on the night shift had had their throats slit and their weapons stolen. Supposedly the robbers all wore V-shaped badges pinned to their upper arms. That's a remarkably stupid way to make yourself conspicuous. Around noon military police inspected our cars, but because we had UNDP-issued badges, they didn't siphon our tanks dry. Old Ma says there are a lot of people running low fevers.

2011-02-02 23:56 Tonight they handed out a yellow medicinal liquid and ordered us to drink it in three doses. Since we'd already been injected with isotopes, none of us drank it, except Blade, who swallowed his without a second thought. Last night it wasn't only the throat-slittings. Several cars were stolen too. We don't dare sleep in the tents anymore, so we've parked our three vehicles pressed tightly side by side in a line, with only the cab on the far left usable for getting in and out. On the iPod we watched Zhao Benshan's old comedy sketches. The final year of them was unexpectedly sad to watch.

2011-02-03 12:55 This morning at the porridge station it was clear noodle soup again, though this time there was actually cabbage in it, and I couldn't decide whether it was safe to eat. A few more medical tents marked with the blue Star of Life have appeared on the main street, and a lot of people are on IV drips. Fortunately there don't seem to have been any deaths yet. The local Salar people are very wary of us outsiders, though they're perfectly cordial to other Qinghai natives. Some people who can speak Qinghai dialect have even managed to move into private homes. I'm going to try my luck too.

2011-02-03 18:04 Something happened to Blade's wife today. During the lunch break she suddenly developed a fever, accompanied by mild convulsions, darting eye movements, and a racing pulse. Blade tried to take her to a medical tent, but the symptoms vanished halfway there. In the afternoon she had two more episodes, both lasting only a little over ten minutes, and afterward she felt completely normal. Blade didn't dare ask a doctor directly. He could only inquire in private whether anyone else had shown similar symptoms and what the consequences might be.

2011-02-03 23:57 Tonight I traded a forty-five-liter Pathfinder backpack to a stranger who also drives a Sahara in exchange for two single-beam laser emitters, shaped like flashlights and capable of firing green laser beams. Supposedly they're especially useful at night, enough to blind someone in an instant. Blade's wife had two more episodes this afternoon, though still no other symptoms. At dusk the military police forced two robbers at the county courthouse, and half the town went to watch.

2011-02-03 23:58 Xunhua is only one hundred and sixty kilometers from Xining. Even with detours it shouldn't be more than two hundred. We agreed to leave tomorrow. We can't stay here forever. We need to keep moving west. Around nine o'clock tonight, army engineering vehicles started rolling down Provincial Road 202. The lead vehicle carried a huge placard reading "Convoy Begins." The line kept roaring past until just now, when the tail vehicle finally appeared, also carrying a big sign, this one with "Convoy Ends" spelled out in glowing batons.

2011-02-04 08:39 Before dawn we were jolted awake by an enormous roar. Outside it was bright as day. Several blinding white arcs rose from a valley more than ten kilometers away and streaked east across the sky. PL said they were Dongfeng-25B medium-range surface-to-surface missiles. I know Second Artillery units of Lanzhou Military Region are deployed in Haidong. No one knows what happened or where those missiles were headed. A moment later several more white trails rose, this time flying southeast.

2011-02-04 08:52 Note: the Dongfeng-25B medium-range conventional surface-to-surface missile, an upgraded version of the Dongfeng-25, also known as the Dongfeng-21C, with an effective range of 3,200 kilometers, enough to cover most targets in Asia. It can carry three warheads but has no guidance module. It uses a five-axle semi-trailer launcher for mobility.

2011-02-05 00:27 I spent the whole day in dread, with images of Judgment Day filling my head. I had never before seen a missile launch so magnificent and so terrifying. The radio says the purpose was to completely destroy the contaminated forests of Shennongjia and Zhangjiajie. But against forests, what good are missiles compared to incendiaries? Even though no formal martial order has been announced, no one in Xunhua dares leave now. Another two engineering convoys passed through this afternoon and evening.

2011-02-05 00:37 Ever since the business in Sangke, there's been a layer between us and Old Ma. Even ordinary conversation has become stiff. I don't want to force myself to act more magnanimous than I feel, but I do hope we can close this gap somehow, because all of us understand more clearly now how important it is to survive together, and we need to know one another better than before. So a strange but somehow sensible thing has started happening: every day everyone competes to take on work, trying to dissolve something unspoken. Blade alone remains stubbornly himself.

2011-02-05 10:34 This morning more than a dozen military trucks rolled into the county town. A man in his fifties wearing a major general's insignia broadcast over the loudspeakers, recruiting construction workers for fortification work in Guide. He wouldn't say exactly what they were building, only that food and lodging were covered, immunity guaranteed, and military goods issued. I suspect those engineering convoys from the past two days were all headed there. Now they must be short of engineers, so they're recruiting civilians. What could they possibly be doing in Guide? That's downstream from Longyang Gorge.

2011-02-05 10:35 None of us intends to go, but many people signed up, especially those with no car and no remaining baggage. The major general said a convoy would come at noon to collect them. The military police under his command were wearing black steel helmets, and on those helmets I saw that familiar emblem again: a soldier in a helmet holding a stalk of wheat. They spread out and began explaining the policies for the construction site. We all hid ourselves away with the UNDP people.

2011-02-06 00:21 It's midnight, and the county town is still thick with shifting headlights, elongated shadows sliding across the endless black backdrop. All day long military trucks have carried people away, only to unload even more, apparently redirected here in an organized way from other jammed provincial roads. The officers recruiting laborers are still moving among the travelers, stressing more and more the guarantee of food. Longyang Gorge. I'd heard stories about it as early as primary school.

2011-02-06 00:36 Blade's wife has now been having these episodes for two full days. Blade says she is generally normal in the car, but more likely to have an attack while standing on sandy ground. It suddenly occurred to us that she is the only one who neither received the isotope injection nor drank the yellow medicine. At dusk we had her drink the dose she'd skipped that day, and sure enough she was much better by evening. Too many new arrivals have crowded into the county town now; even the open ground beside our cars is full, and things have become oppressively cramped. Even going out to relieve yourself means a long walk.

2011-02-06 14:23 Today more travelers, though by now they probably ought to be called drifters, kept pouring in. From Qingshui to Jiezi the place was packed with people, and the banks of the Yellow River were covered in tents of every bright color. Soot-streaked, dust-caked people could do little besides chop wood and drink boiled river water; most survived only on relief from the porridge kitchens. We decided to leave here and head west along the Yellow River. If all went well, once past Lijia Gorge we could take the Xita Expressway straight to Xining.

2011-02-06 15:51 UNDP's bottled drinking water had long since run out. My wife was helping distribute a simple water-filtering device that let people drink straight from the Yellow River. It was just a plastic tube containing seven layers of filter mesh; short of heavy-metal contamination, it could purify any water source. Unfortunately the supply was tiny, at most only a few thousand units. My wife was told that once they were all handed out, her appointment would end as well, and someone kindly advised her to move farther upstream.

2011-02-06 16:05 I went to the temporary military service depot to buy matches and unexpectedly ran into my middle-school classmate Gajian. He's from Ledu, and his whole family fled to his grandmother's place in Xunhua on January 20. I asked why he hadn't headed toward Xining, and he said, "Who still goes to the cities now? Our house is already full of relatives who ran from Xining and Lanzhou." I was stunned. Wasn't Lanzhou supposed to be the "rear area" on the radio? If even the locals had fled, what kind of scene must the city be in now?

2011-02-06 19:22 After my wife finished up a little after five, we drove out of the county town. Old Ma hadn't gone back to work for UNDP yesterday; he said the job was too dangerous. When we reached Yimamu Bridge, we found that only military vehicles were allowed through. So we kept following the Yellow River bank. The road was swarming with people, so chaotic that we couldn't build up any speed at all. We crept all the way to Zanzhongzhuang only to find that we couldn't cross the river there either. They couldn't seriously expect us to take sheepskin rafts, could they?

2011-02-06 19:38 While we were hesitating by the bridge about whether to keep going or what to do, we ran into Gajian again. He was driving a Great Wall pickup back to his home on Gandu Street. After seeing our situation, he whispered a few words to the sentry on the bridge, then waved grandly for us to follow him, and we crossed without trouble. Only then did I realize that the guards on this bridge were Hui and Salar militiamen, wearing green badges painted with a star and crescent.

2011-02-06 19:48 Gajian said it was already so late that we might as well stay at his place. His grandmother's home on Gandu Street was a large riverside compound, with a front courtyard big enough to serve as a parking lot for more than twenty cars. Back in school Gajian had always been low-key and honest; I never imagined his family was this well-off. Plenty of vehicles were already parked in the courtyard. Besides local plates, there were several off-roaders from Sichuan and Shaanxi too, apparently other outsiders like us.

2011-02-07 00:03 We were settled into three single-story rooms in the west yard. It had been so long since we'd slept in a warm house. Freshly washed socks were drying on the heater pipes, and outside, parachute cord was strung from car to car with clothes that had been washed all evening hanging from it. We spoke with Gajian and confirmed it would be no problem for us to stay two days. Even though his family certainly wouldn't mention money, we had to show something. We scraped together a roll of dollars from Blade and pressed it on him. He didn't refuse.

2011-02-07 00:41 Gajian's grandmother's household runs a farmhouse inn, which explains why there are so many rooms. I had just gone to the toilet and tripped over something; when I got up I realized it was a person lying on the ground. I helped her up and found it was a woman staying in the next room. When I helped open her door, I saw she was by herself, having made it here alone all the way from Chengdu. Remarkable, really. From the way she described it, her symptoms were the same as Blade's wife's.

2011-02-07 00:48 There are actually militiamen standing watch around Gajian's courtyard, three to a group, and they're well equipped. He explained that after the situation turned chaotic, nearby people of the same community had formed a collective. I know the ethnic makeup here is complicated, with Salar, Hui, Tibetan, and even Bonan and Dongxiang communities, each with its own groups. It's hard to believe such disciplined paramilitary forces were assembled in so short a time. They must have existed all along.

2011-02-07 11:39 This morning I finally had a satisfying bath in the yard's washroom, using water pumped straight from the Yellow River. After eating two kunguo flatbreads, I carried a little folding stool into the courtyard and chatted away with the other travelers. There were people from Chengdu, Mianyang, Guangyuan, Xi'an, Baoji, and Tianshui. It felt very much like years ago in a little guesthouse in Motuo, talking with strangers. Conditions were much the same everywhere; only Mianyang still had garrison troops. In Shaanxi, many people had evacuated toward Inner Mongolia.

2011-02-07 11:48 The woman I met last night was there too. She wore an olive quick-dry shirt, and the exposed stretch of one arm was covered in scars. Her Twitter handle, @leftry, was embroidered on her cuff. Her companions had all been lost on the road, and only she had made it this far. Mentioning Twitter reminded me to prop up the VSAT from the car and get online. Twitter wouldn't open. I tried sending a message through a client I'd built myself, but it failed. Those base stations must all be down.

2011-02-07 16:09 Though Gandu lies only a river away from Xunhua county town, its streets are much quieter, with none of the dust-colored drifters in sight. The white courtyard walls glare under the plateau sun. Sitting in the yard, I can see a tall tower capped with a crescent spire, and even a lama temple in a hybrid Han-Tibetan style. At noon I slipped out alone to spin the prayer wheels and light two butter lamps. Afraid of offending Gajian's family, I didn't go straight back, and instead strolled along the river for a while.

2011-02-07 16:30 More than a dozen large SUVs were jammed at the bridgehead we crossed yesterday, all luxury models like Lexuses. The people inside were waving submachine guns, trying to force their way across, but the bridge guards refused to yield. Dozens of guns came up in unison, and there were even rocket launchers. After a tense standoff, a Q7 with Lanzhou plates drove up and led the convoy back toward Yimamu Bridge to cross there instead. Then I suddenly spotted a familiar vehicle at the tail end: that Hummer H5.

2011-02-08 00:06 Toward evening I went to the lama temple again, hoping to ask the geshe for some Tibetan medicine. Instead I found that the convoy of luxury SUVs that had crossed the river this afternoon had settled there. Three of the vehicles carried military plates. There had to be twenty or thirty people in thirteen cars, yet only a dozen or so were drawing water and cooking; the rest never got out. They all wore hoods and monk-like robes, and several even had gas masks on. The bald man was among them too.

2011-02-08 00:12 Afraid the bald man would spot me, I backed out. But I had already realized that this convoy must have been assembled piece by piece along the road, because the plates were from different places and the conditions of the cars and the wear on the tires didn't match. But the military vehicle that had led them across the bridge had to belong to real military people, and important ones at that. Otherwise how could it possibly have passed Yimamu Bridge? The whole thing was baffling: they were so secretive, and staying here of all places.

2011-02-08 00:15 That evening Gajian invited me to his family's dinner. I grew up in the Muslim quarter of Dongguan in Xining, and I even had a distant grandmother from a Hui family, so I still know some of the etiquette. Gajian's elder brother Naibao asked me in great detail about what we'd seen along the way. When he finished listening, he said that Xining and Chengdu were not the same, and that they were preparing a convoy to return there, because in the coming months Xining would become even safer.

2011-02-08 00:26 I asked in astonishment why, but he would say no more, only that the Dongguan Great Mosque was a place under Allah's protection. After dinner, though, Gajian quietly told me that this had to do with military engineering works in the upper Yellow River. Supposedly they were carrying out a quarantine measure called "Green Dam" to secure the water sources upstream. Once that happened, the banks downstream from Lijia Gorge would become unsafe, and Lanzhou itself might even be abandoned.

2011-02-08 00:26 Xining, by contrast, depends on the Huangshui River, which has strong natural self-filtration, and it was not directly struck by the meteor shower. The earlier wave of retreat had been caused entirely by mass panic. West of Xining there is only boundless Gobi and the dried-up Qinghai Lake; apart from Golmud, hundreds of kilometers away, there is no major region left where humans can survive. To go farther west would be suicide. So they had decided to set out within two days and return to Xining.

2011-02-08 00:32 After I went back and discussed it with the others, we quickly decided to go with Gajian's group, since it matched our plan anyway. To my surprise, he refused flatly. His reason was that, for certain religious reasons, his family would not travel together with outsiders. But he added that we could follow far behind them. That made little practical difference, so I agreed and thanked him. We would stay here two more days. What exactly was this "Green Dam" quarantine plan?

2011-02-08 13:59 At noon we sat in the courtyard and ate a proper bowl of lamb mianpian. I noticed @leftry sitting off by herself and quietly asked whether she would like to come with us. I'd asked Gajian about her; the reason she could stay here was that she had long traded dark tea between Qinghai and Hunan and knew many of the local merchants well. She looked at me in surprise, and after making sure I wasn't joking, happily agreed. I arranged for PL to share a car with her.

2011-02-08 14:09 Of course, I had my own calculations. It was inconvenient for PL to stay crammed together with Old Ma's family, so it would be better to split him off. And if @leftry knew the merchants here, chances were she knew people in other parts of Haidong as well. With the future so uncertain, one more person meant one more chance. Besides, the fact that she was the only one of her companions to survive surely wasn't mere coincidence. She drove a battered old Jetta and didn't mind taking one more passenger. PL wore a blank expression and made no comment.

2011-02-08 22:21 The lama temple outside has caught fire. Everyone is helping put it out. For all that these different groups normally keep apart, almost like fire and water, when trouble comes they don't hesitate. There's no relying on a fire brigade here; the town has its own civilian mutual-aid fire teams. We're helping drag hoses.

2011-02-08 23:25 Fortunately the fire only burned one of the temple's side buildings. It was probably just someone cooking a late-night meal and letting it get out of hand. The SUV convoy that had been in the courtyard was already gone; I have no idea when they left. The lamas and Tibetan locals were clearing the fire scene, and the militia withdrew of their own accord to avoid giving offense. Near the flowerbeds in the courtyard I saw a great deal of gauze thrown aside, as though it had just been unwound from patients' bodies, stiff with dried blood.

2011-02-08 23:33 The lamas carried strange charred bones out of the burned building, just barely identifiable as having four limbs, and giving off a nauseating scorched smell. I didn't know what they were. Probably human, I guessed, yet in many ways inconsistent with human skeletons. More and more Tibetans living nearby were arriving, so I withdrew uneasily. We are leaving here first thing tomorrow morning. Gajian's group will set out before noon, and we have been told to leave ahead of them.

2011-02-09 11:23 On the road again. Setting out once more. This route is somewhat more familiar, but because of the season and the altitude, supplies will be even harder to find. There are still ten days left until Spring Festival. The other travelers staying in Gajian's courtyard were also turned out. Lacking preparation, some went looking for other shelter, while others fell in behind our convoy. We plan to drive a few miles ahead and wait in place for Gajian's convoy to pass.

2011-02-09 13:11 After waiting more than two hours, we finally saw dust rising on the road and a convoy hurtling toward us. There were more than twenty vehicles, all kinds of them, including even a black riot-control truck. At the front and rear were armored cash vans. Once the convoy had passed, we hung far behind it. I found their frequency on the radio; it wasn't encrypted, but they were speaking in Xunhua dialect mixed with Arabic, making them very hard to understand.

2011-02-09 15:24 We had not driven long along Provincial Road 202 before, near Hequn Reservoir outside Hualong, we saw the Salar convoy come to a stop in the distance. Through binoculars we could see that local Hui militiamen had set up a roadblock. Hualong is a Hui autonomous county. Though they follow the same religion, the two sides did not seem to be getting along very well. After arguing for a while, the convoy drove down off the roadbed and turned left onto Provincial Road 203 to the west. They were evidently going to bypass Hualong.

2011-02-09 15:28 Once the convoy ahead had gone, the Hui militia from Hualong quickly spotted us, and several pickups belching black smoke came charging our way. I knew that in a place and time like this there was no point trying to reason with anyone, so I shouted for everyone to turn around and floored the accelerator. They gave symbolic chase for a few kilometers, fired several shots into the air, and then turned back. We found a small road leading west and felt our way toward Provincial Road 203.

2011-02-09 16:23 The eight counties of Haidong are the most prosperous part of Qinghai. Even the country houses are fairly decent; at the very least you don't see any of those old earthen-brick dwellings. Because of the historical influence of Han settlers who moved inland here, many courtyards have the habit of keeping flowerbeds. But because of the climate the fields are utterly barren, and the rolling mountains show nothing but dry yellow-brown soil. Only the patches of white mottling on the poplars remind us that the plants here are all dead.

2011-02-09 22:49 A little after five in the afternoon we caught sight of the Xunhua convoy again. They had planned to take Provincial Road 203 to the Ping'a Expressway, but near Zhaba they ran into another group of local armed men. With no alternative, they turned back again and continued west, moving along the Yellow River toward Provincial Road 101. Once they reached Huangzhong they could take the Xita Expressway into Xining. But that area is traditional Tibetan turf, and if the Xunhua convoy was already this tense with the Hui of Hualong, then what awaited them in Huangzhong was even harder to predict.

2011-02-09 23:15 That night we stayed in a village halfway up the mountain north of Lijia Gorge. The Xunhua convoy was in another village only a few hundred meters away. Though this place is close to both Xunhua and Hualong, the mountain villages here were strangely abandoned, without a single villager. The magnificent Lijia Gorge Dam blazed with lights, guarded all around by People's Armed Police. From afar we could see warning lines glowing orange. We didn't even dare light a campfire, for fear of drawing trouble.

2011-02-09 23:40 We picked a random farmhouse, and everything inside was still there: bedding, household things, as if the people had left in a hurry. We had just fallen asleep when Old Ma's wife came knocking to say that @leftry was having another episode. Luckily we still had some of the yellow medicine left over from before, so we gave her a bottle to drink. Blade's wife has been all right these past few days, though she has grown somewhat dull. A Salar scout stopped by to look us over that night and tacitly accepted our shadowing them.

2011-02-10 09:13 Daybreak. From here we could overlook the grand sweep of Lijia Gorge Reservoir. The Yellow River here ought still to have been a clear, gleaming expanse of blue-green water. At the moment it wasn't muddy, yet it somehow looked lifeless. Through binoculars I could see armed-police engineering troops busy on the dam. Tanker trucks stood in a long line, pouring yellow liquid from the dam into the reservoir. I have no idea what they were doing. Could they really be trying to disinfect the entire reservoir? It seemed hopelessly inadequate.

2011-02-10 13:48 North of Lijia Gorge the mountain country is all Tibetan townships. Fortunately no one was left here; otherwise the Salar convoy, which their scout insisted last night we refer to by the full name "Salar," would probably have run into serious trouble. This stretch of road was hard going, and it was noon before we reached Ashigong and got onto Provincial Road 101. Farther west lay Longyang Gorge. The Salar convoy stopped here to cook, while we refilled our water by the river. We were running low on fuel.

2011-02-10 21:18 Like the Chengdu-Mianyang Expressway, the Xita Expressway was completely blocked by abandoned vehicles and shell craters. It was impossible to pass. So we followed the Salar convoy along the old road northward. Here and there you could still see occupied houses with lights on, and the air smelled of butter tea. Old Ma's car ran out of fuel, so we picked a house and made camp there, then took jerrycans and drove Blade's car up to the Xita Expressway to siphon gasoline from the tanks of abandoned vehicles.

2011-02-11 07:17 In the middle of the night we were awakened by dense gunfire and explosions. We stood in the courtyard peering out. It seemed the Salar convoy was exchanging fire with someone. Both sides had heavy weapons. We didn't light a single lamp, afraid of exposing ourselves, like children watching fireworks in the dark. The fighting lasted nearly two hours before ending. It looked as though Gajian's people had lost. Night swallowed everything. None of us slept again. We kept watch in every direction.

2011-02-11 09:06 At first light, Blade and I took our guns, found a pedal tricycle at the farmhouse, and cautiously rode over to the site of the battle. The riot-control truck had had its roof blown off and was smoking with a scorched, foul farmyard stench. The Salar convoy had been wiped out to the last person; even the women and children had been spared no mercy. Whoever attacked them in the night struck hard and had plenty of firepower. It was pure revenge, because they hadn't even bothered to clear the battlefield. The two of us picked through the dead for useful things.

2011-02-11 13:14 In the morning we planned to observe the situation around us before moving on, especially since Xining was already close. But very quickly a small squad of Tibetan soldiers found us and surrounded us with two Land Cruisers and some motorcycles. While we were still at a loss, @leftry explained in halting Tibetan that we were refugees from Sichuan. I hurriedly showed them the Buddhist beads hanging from my medal ribbon and the khata in the car, and as it happened I really did have a khata blessed by a living Buddha from Kumbum Monastery.

2011-02-11 13:21 The Tibetan soldiers said a few words about being careful and were just about to leave when they spotted the guns we had picked up that morning lying on the ground. Instantly they all raised their weapons again. Blade's Tibetan has a Jinchuan accent, and he hurried to explain that we had only picked up a few firearms for self-defense, though I don't know whether they understood him. In a few swift moves the soldiers confiscated our pistols too. After speaking over the radio for quite a while, several of them jumped into our vehicles and said they were taking us to Kumbum Monastery.

2011-02-11 14:46 The cars had not driven long before they stopped, short of the monastery itself. In the distance I could see a great golden roof flashing brilliantly in the sun. This was a small town on the outskirts of Lushaer county, and at last we understood why all the Tibetan villages along the way had been empty: everyone had gathered around the monasteries, settling in an orderly way into farmhouses or tent camps in groups. Old Ma's wife, PL, and the others, who couldn't stand the smell of butter tea, all covered their noses.

2011-02-11 14:51 We were left sitting in the cars with the keys removed and no one paying us any attention. Two Tibetan soldiers kept watch over us with their guns while chatting with nearby villagers. The local children gathered around to stare at us curiously, but without hostility. One Tibetan woman even brought us a pot of butter tea, which moved me almost to tears. But why were they detaining us? In the distance came the low, mournful blare of dungchen horns. People prostrating themselves in long bows were everywhere.

2011-02-11 22:01 It was fully dark. We were eating boiled cured pork with butter tea when at last a group of Tibetan soldiers arrived escorting a high lama. We put on our most pitiful expressions and explained ourselves at length. After checking our ID cards, the lama agreed to let us go, but advised us not to enter Xining. The Tibetans near Kumbum Monastery were evacuating in an organized way toward Yushu, and the resourceful monks of Kumbum had even managed to secure a train that would depart west along the Qinghai-Tibet line for Tibet.

2011-02-11 22:21 Since the neighbors here were friendly, we decided to stay the night. Even if we could still reach Xining, charging into a silent city in pitch darkness would not be wise. Leftry suggested that instead of doing that, why not simply head into Tibet? If we missed this train, we would never get another chance. She was right. Blade went after the high lama to ask when and where the train would depart. Before that, though, if time allowed, we still wanted to take a look at Xining.

2011-02-11 22:58 Blade found out that the train would leave from Duoba on the afternoon of the 13th, but the number of passengers it could take was extremely limited, and there was no guarantee the railway ahead was even intact. Many of the men had chosen on their own to drive or even go on foot, giving the places to women and children. Others had no intention of leaving at all, especially the devout believers prostrating themselves beneath the bodhi tree. Tibetan Buddhism is about the next life. Blade really is impressive: he even managed to get back the pistol that had been confiscated.

2011-02-12 10:46 When we woke in the morning, more than half the Tibetan tents around us were already empty. From afar came waves of chanting, and the crowds had all converged on the square at Kumbum Monastery. We packed up and left without a word.
