> Get as close as possible to the object of your desire. — The LOMO Rules

Some people say that the character *cheng* in Chengdu is really a pictograph of an electric scooter. If you have ever seen the flood of scooters during rush hour, you may think the joke is not entirely wrong.

It was already midnight, and Lin Fei could finally ride without restraint. Even so, the dark figure proved faster than he had imagined. He could barely keep up. But like a cat teasing its prey, the shadow always paused for him at just the crucial turning points. Even so, any ordinary man would have lost it long ago. Fortunately Lin Fei had another camera in his backpack: a Polaroid SX-70 Alpha 1 disguised to look like an elegant leather notebook.

Polaroid cameras, born in 1937, were the pioneers of instant photography. The moment he pressed the shutter, a square of Type 700 film slid warm from the bottom of the camera. Truth be told, with Polaroid long out of production and 700 film close to impossible to find, every step he took and every shutter click cost him real pain. By the time he had reached the First Ring Road, he had already burned through over a hundred yuan.

"What a monster," he muttered. "This is pricier than taking a cab. Mendenger had better reimburse me." But he kept riding, checking each still-warm instant photo as it developed and following the dark figure through the city.

With his own eyes he watched the thief eat and steal in a single motion. First came the famous beef-noodle hotpot at Xinhua Park, where he also made off with the copper pot of old broth. Then on Shuijingfang's cold-snack street he stole a bronze wine vat full of plum liquor. From there he went to a wonton shop by Qingshiqiao and carried off the great jar of fermented broad-bean paste. Then, changing direction again, he ran to Zhang Duck and Mouse Hole near Niuwangmiao and stole the braising cauldron used for marinated duck. Before leaving, he even altered the restaurant's signboard from "cool cup" to the proper Chengdu spelling "cold-snack cup."

Panting, Lin Fei followed him all the way to Mingtin Restaurant in Caojia Lane, only to find that the figure had finally stopped. There he sat in the empty main room, bold as brass, with a whole heap of pots, kettles, jars, and basins piled at his feet. Add to them the little steamer used for Mingtin's braised tofu and brains, and there were exactly eight items in all. They differed in size, but every one bore three handles and *taotie* patterns. Clearly they had all come from the same lineage.

Only now did Lin Fei truly see the thief's face. He was fatter even than Mendenger, with pale skin but hands and ears black as soot, a little tuft of dark hair atop his head, and dark birthmarks circling his eyes like bruises. He was, in a word, spectacularly ugly.

Lin Fei tried to match this sorry face with the *taotie* he remembered. Apart from the same oversized body, the resemblance was not obvious. Carefully he asked, "You... why did you stop running?"

The fat man was busy annihilating the dishes on the table: tofu and brains, scallion-baked kidneys, duck intestines in bean soup, pork rib and kelp broth. Mingtin might have looked run-down, but in Chengdu it was one of the greatest *fly restaurants* of them all, perfect fusion of the city's shabbiness and the city's genius for food. Chengdu is that kind of place. People care more about pleasure than prestige. If you ever see someone arrive in a Porsche to eat hot-and-sour noodles in an alley, do not be surprised.

While slurping down another bite of brains, the fat man wagged his head and said, "Couldn't carry any more. You chased too close, so I stopped to have a proper meal."

"Who are you, exactly?" Lin Fei asked. "Are you related to the *taotie*?"

"That ugly brute?" the fat man snapped. "You're the one related to him. Your whole family is related to him."

"Hey, no need to start insulting people. Let's talk properly. What's your name? And why are you stealing all these three-handled pots?"

The fat man set down his chopsticks and gave him a look of contempt. "Aren't you the famous Mr. Lomo, Dingcat himself? You're the one who organized the public LOMO wall project on Dingding Cat last time. I even went to see it. And you don't recognize me?" Dingcat was Lin Fei's online handle, as well as his ID on Dingding Cat, the Ancient Shu secret forum named after his father, Old Dingcat.

"You know the Dingding Cat forum?" Lin Fei asked in astonishment.

"Of course. I'm a senior member."

Dingding Cat was closed-registration only. It did not have many members, but Lin Fei certainly had not met them all in person. "You can't be Ancient Shu," he said. "I know all the Shu people."

"Correct," the fat man said. "Because I really am not human."

Lin Fei's mind raced. The forum only had so many users. If this man was not Shu, then he had to be one of the auspicious beasts, forest spirits, or some other such being. He looked again at the dark rings around the man's eyes and suddenly felt a jolt.

"Don't tell me..." he said. "You're a *pixiu*?"

The fat man patted his belly with satisfaction and beckoned him closer. "That's more like it. You should've guessed sooner. My name is Qu Mao'er. Come, sit down and eat."

> Don't think. Act fast. — The LOMO Rules

Qu Mao'er was not just using *pixiu* as a handle, nor was he pretending to be one. He truly was a pixiu. In ancient Chinese lore, the pixiu is an auspicious beast with male and female forms; in the south both are generally called pixiu, while in the north the creature is also known as *bixie*. Across many dynasties the imperial guards wore pixiu heads on their armor and were known as the Pixiu Guard. At some point in history, someone captured several ferocious bear-like beasts in Guanzhong and presented them to the emperor. The emperor's courtiers did not know what they were and simply invented an answer, declaring them celestial pixiu. That became the official name.

In reality, however, pixiu were never fierce. They had sharp teeth and claws, yes, but they did not eat meat. In 1869 the French missionary Armand David rediscovered them in Sichuan and named them "black-and-white bears." Two years later they were further labeled *mao xiong*, "cat bears." In the 1940s, when a specimen exhibition was held at the Beibei Museum in Chongqing, the Chinese habit of reading certain signs from right to left caused *mao xiong* to be misread as *xiong mao*. From that point onward the now-familiar term "panda" spread everywhere through a simple error.

Qu Mao'er, then, was a panda genuine beyond doubt. In Chengdu dialect, however, *qu mao'er* means frog. He had chosen the name only to express a humble wish of his own: to have a color photograph taken.

Sure enough, after only a few mouthfuls he made his request. "Dingcat, I hear LOMO photos come out with dazzling colors. Take a few of me later, will you?"

"With your complexion? You'll be one color no matter what lens I use. First explain why you stole Sanxingdui's bronze ding." Lin Fei glared at him. "You're an old hand on the forum. You know Sanxingdui is the foundation of Shu culture, our root and bone. So why touch it at all? And these pots and jars too." He pointed at the eight vessels on the floor. "The little newspaper hacks of Chengdu thrive on gossip. Heaven knows what kind of uproar tomorrow's papers will make of this."

"You really don't know?" Qu Mao'er said, looking heartbroken. "Do you remember that year when all the bamboo in Sichuan flowered and we pandas nearly starved to death?"

"Of course I do. There was no internet yet, and all the Chengdu old hands raised donations. You certainly ate plenty of our special relief rations."

"But the thing that saved the bamboo in the end was neither you nor modern science. The news said it was a climate cycle, but I know the truth. That time it really was the *taotie* that made the difference. He took rubbings of the *taotie* patterns on the paired bronze dings from Sanxingdui and used them to bring the bamboo back."

"I thought the pictographs on those dings were supposed to be a recipe book."

"Wrong. What they actually recorded was an Ancient Shu materia text called *The Shu Arts*. One section dealt specifically with curing bamboo. If Sanxingdui had never been excavated, you Shu folk would have had your peace and we'd have had an easier time borrowing what we needed. Ever since one of the dings was broken, the complete text of *The Shu Arts* has been scattered among the people. Luckily your father forced through a decision to take rubbings before that happened."

"So this time you stole them because..."

"Because I had no choice," Qu Mao'er said. "And to reassure you lot, I even fetched another piece from a different Sanxingdui site and left it there to calm you down."

"What? There's another Sanxingdui?" Lin Fei nearly jumped out of his chair.

"Easy, easy. That's another matter. This time it's because the rabbits are in trouble."

"The rabbits? What rabbits?"

"You know why Chengdu people love eating rabbit? Think of those famous rabbit heads in Shuangliu."

Lin Fei licked his lips before answering. "Because in legend, the rabbit came from what King Wen of Zhou vomited up after he had been tricked into eating patties made from the flesh of his own eldest son, Bo Yikao. And since the Shu kingdom was betrayed and destroyed by the Zhou, later generations of Shu people longed to eat their enemy alive. That's why Bo Yikao's incarnation met such treatment."

"Exactly." Qu Mao'er drew a hand across his throat. "And in such a brutal fashion too. Though to be fair, rabbit heads really are delicious."

"We've let go of that hatred ages ago. I'm on the board of the Chengdu Anti-Cruelty to Livestock Association, remember? Get to the point. What do you mean the rabbits are in trouble?"

"Well, rabbits come in domestic and wild kinds. What we eat now are farmed rabbits. Their living conditions are tolerable. Wild rabbits, though, have it hard. The Chengdu Plain is almost entirely occupied by humans now. There's barely any wilderness left for them. Under the city itself there are countless wild-rabbit settlements. Those were part of a relief project started years ago by the panda folk. Chengdu's old name is Rongcheng, the city of hibiscus, and beneath the ground there grows a fungus called *ronggu*. The rabbits live on that now."

"And what does that have to do with *The Shu Arts*? Does it also include animal protection?"

Qu Mao'er raised his brows. "Are you even from Chengdu? Haven't you heard they're building the subway? Seven lines in all, tearing the underground to pieces."

"Damn," Lin Fei said, slapping his thigh. "Have they destroyed the rabbits' warrens? Wait, does *The Shu Arts* contain a secret method for sabotaging the subway?"

"Don't interrupt. We already meddled with the subway business. All the lines now detour around the rabbit warrens."

"No wonder people online keep complaining the routes make no sense. So that's your doing."

"But now we've got a new problem. The subway has cut Chengdu's underground water veins. The *ronggu* won't grow anymore, and the rabbits have nothing to eat." Qu Mao'er spread both hands helplessly. "We've already planned dozens of new rabbit settlements in Xindu, Jintang, Pixian, and Qionglai, places unlikely to get subways for a century. But *ronggu* doesn't grow there. Taotie says *The Shu Arts* contains cultivation methods for over a dozen mushrooms and cruciferous plants — the old art is nearly lost. So I meant to piece it back together, learn from it, and plant something suitable under the new settlements to save the rabbits."

"Qu Mao'er," Lin Fei said gravely, patting him on the shoulder, "you are a good comrade. But taking other people's things without permission is still wrong. How are these restaurant owners supposed to make a living?"

"I left them replacement cookware, didn't I? Pure gold! I've taken a serious loss already."

"Gold is too soft to put over a fire, you idiot. Here's what we'll do. I'll photograph every pattern, we'll stitch them together and study them at leisure, and afterward you return all the cookware to where it belongs."

"Deal." Qu Mao'er slapped him so hard on the back that Lin Fei nearly pitched forward. "But this is urgent. Let's photograph everything now and deliver it back tonight."

> Arrange the shots however you like. You don't need to know beforehand what you'll get. — The LOMO Rules

Lin Fei and Qu Mao'er returned to Lin Fei's place, developed the photographs of the bronze ding and the eight other vessels, scanned them into the computer, and stitched them together with a program called AutoStitch. At last the complete text of *The Shu Arts* emerged.

Lin Fei posted an announcement on Dingding Cat about the rabbit crisis, and many Shu people immediately volunteered to help cultivate new food sources. He then called Mendenger and told him to come by the next day to retrieve the bronze ding, while not failing to remind him about reimbursement. Only after everything was settled did Lin Fei breathe out in relief and hand Qu Mao'er the rubbings relating to fungi and crucifer cultivation.

Qu Mao'er thanked him effusively and tucked the rubbings into his coat. "This time it's all thanks to you. At least now the complete *Shu Arts* has been preserved. Even if the originals are lost again, I can still consult the text."

"And I should thank Brother Pixiu. Without you, I never would've known any of this."

"Then I won't waste any more time. I need to head to the rabbit settlements and start experimenting. Bye-bye."

He turned to go, but Lin Fei caught him by the arm. "Wait. Can I ask you something?"

"Ask away."

"I thought pandas only ate bamboo. How is it you can eat absolutely everything?"

As Lin Fei asked, he stepped closer. Qu Mao'er suddenly spun toward the window to make a break for it. Lin Fei's hand shot out and grabbed the long furry tail protruding from his trouser leg. He squeezed hard.

"Miaow!" Qu Mao'er shrieked. "All right, I confess! I'm not a panda at all! I'm just a black-and-white cat, that's all, miaow..."

"You fraud. I'll knock your ears off."

At midnight, the empty street echoed with the shouts, thuds, and curses of a man and a cat chasing and fighting each other through the dark.
