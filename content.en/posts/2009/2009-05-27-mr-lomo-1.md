> Carry it with you. — The LOMO Rules

The ease of old Chengdu was never just something people said. It lived in the bones. That native calmness and composure kept every part of the city in a state of almost lazy comfort. Even white-collar office workers hurrying through the forest of steel and concrete moved more slowly than their counterparts in other cities.

Lin Fei lived in Yulin, one of Chengdu's most leisurely neighborhoods. He was a lanky man, an HR officer for a headhunting firm, forever dressed in a stand-collar athletic top with an overstuffed backpack slung over one shoulder. Whenever he spoke with clients, he always stressed that the *Fei* in his name was pronounced the third tone rather than the first, so that no one would laugh at how feminine it sounded. Every evening after work, he passed through the Tiaosanta intersection, walked west into Yulin East Street, and turned right at the first parasol tree he saw. Beneath it stood a tiny bobo chicken shop called Jinmantang, so cramped that a handful of crooked little tables and stools nearly filled the place. If you wanted to eat there, you picked your skewers from the fridge, shouted "Boss!" and someone would bring out a basin of red chili oil and set it before you. Lin Fei always ordered a large handful of spicy beef skewers and two liang of dumplings, and that took care of dinner in perfect comfort.

Today, after work, he came to Jinmantang as usual. He ordered a pile of food and sat down. Unlike other evenings, however, there was already a second dipping dish set out across from him, though the seat remained empty until Lin Fei had built up a little mound of bamboo skewers beside his bowl. Only then did a plump middle-aged man waddle into the shop and sit down opposite him.

"Sorry, sorry. Had to pick up the kid from school. Couldn't help it," the fat man said, snatching up a duck tongue skewer, dipping it in oil, and stuffing it into his mouth. After two chews he found the taste insufficient, turned his head, and shouted, "Boss, give me a dry dip too!" When the waiter brought over a saucer of dried chili and Sichuan pepper powder, he finally settled into eating with visible satisfaction.

Lin Fei patiently watched him wipe out the entire basin of bobo chicken and clean the grease from the corners of his mouth. Only then did he ask slowly, "So what is it, Mendenger? What kind of business made you come all the way out here looking for me?"

The man known as Mendenger gave a sly grin. "What, I can't come eat at Jinmantang unless I need something from you?" He pulled a kraft-paper envelope from inside his coat and handed it over. "The Elder lost something. He wants you to help find it."

Lin Fei frowned and tore open the envelope. Inside were several close-up photographs of bronze objects, obviously national-treasure-grade relics. "These are from Sanxingdui, aren't they?" he asked.

The Sanxingdui site lies at Nanxing in Guanghan, Sichuan, only thirty-three kilometers from Chengdu. It got its name from three abrupt earthen mounds rising from the Chengdu Plain. It is a nationally protected heritage site at least four thousand years old. Vast numbers of bronze objects, jade artifacts, and gold pieces have been unearthed there, revealing to the world both the advanced sophistication and the distinctive style of the bronze civilization of the ancient Shu kingdom.

"That's right. None of these have ever been publicly displayed. Look at this one, Storage Room Three." Mendenger jabbed a thick finger at one of the pictures. It showed a display case holding a bronze statue about sixty centimeters high. Lin Fei leaned toward the shop entrance to catch the light more clearly and peered at it. "A protruding-eye figure from Sanxingdui, about forty-nine hundred years old. Looks genuine. So what's the problem?" Sanxingdui yielded no end of artifacts obsessed with eyes, in bizarre forms. *Huayang Guo Zhi* even records that "the Marquis Cancong of Shu had vertical eyes and was the first to call himself king," evidence of Sanxingdui's cult of the eye. In the photograph, the figure's eyeballs bulged out enormously, with the pupils thrust forward like the barrel of a telephoto lens.

"The problem is that this is not what used to be in that room," Mendenger said, one word at a time. "And Sanxingdui Museum doesn't even own this piece, even though it really was excavated at Sanxingdui. The room originally held a great three-handled bronze ding. So tell me: who steals one real relic and leaves another real relic behind? And in ordinary people's eyes, this protruding-eye statue is worth more than that old cauldron."

Lin Fei narrowed his eyes. "Interesting. I thought the Elder had retired. Why is he still meddling in this?" The Elder and Mendenger were both of Lin Fei's people. The Elder had been their previous clan head.

Mendenger grew solemn. "Don't forget, kid. Dead or alive, we are still the last of the Shu."

Lin Fei gave an embarrassed laugh. "No need to get so serious. Relax. I'll look into it properly."

"Did you bring your tools?" Mendenger asked, still not at ease. "The Elder is really anxious this time."

"Of course." Lin Fei patted his backpack. "First rule of LOMO: carry it with you."

> Use it anytime, day or night. — The LOMO Rules

The "Shu people" Mendenger spoke of were not modern Sichuanese. They were the Ancient Shu, the people who had lived here more than three thousand years ago. As early as the Shang dynasty, these large-eyed, high-nosed, square-jawed, broad-eared people had established a highly developed theocratic kingdom here: the ancient state of Shu. Its reach covered what are now Sichuan and Yunnan, and may once even have extended as far as western Hubei and the Qiang territories. Around the time the Shang fell and the Zhou rose, the Shu armies that had helped the Zhou conquer the Shang were themselves turned on and destroyed by the Zhou. From that point on, the Ancient Shu passed from great power into a long age of wandering decline.

Yet they were not exterminated. Their remnants remained scattered across the Chengdu Plain, clinging stubbornly to life, guarding the bloodline and inheritance left by their ancestors. They watched the waves of northerners who migrated south in each new dynasty, merged with them, and quietly altered them from within. Generation after generation of outsiders came into Sichuan and were eventually assimilated into true Shu people, inheriting Shu customs and the Shu cast of mind. Take modern Chengdu residents, for example. Most are descendants of the migrants who entered Sichuan during the great "Huguang fills Sichuan" migrations of two centuries ago, yet their grammar still preserves the unusual Shu structure of subject-object-verb, and when saying "don't know," they often use *xiao bu de*. That is the lingering effect of the Shu language's distinctive "leftward speech."

Lin Fei himself was one of these pure-blooded Ancient Shu. He stayed in contact with others of his kind through a forum called Dingding Cat, an underground network for mutual aid and for guarding and passing down the cultural traces of the old Shu kingdom. The errand Mendenger had brought him was a troublesome one. Sanxingdui, after all, is a nest of unanswered mysteries. Officially, nearly all the bronzes unearthed there are said to be ritual objects, with virtually no everyday utensils among them. In truth, some practical objects did survive; they were simply never made public for certain reasons. This never-disclosed bronze ding was one of them. It was the foundational ritual vessel of ancient Shu food culture, and the long, unbroken lineage of Sichuan cuisine had depended on it for millennia. Who would steal such a thing?

Ancient bronzes are not especially highly priced on the international antiquities black market. If a smuggler had really managed to enter a tightly guarded, undisclosed storage room, then publicly displayed treasures like the Sanxingdui gold scepter would have been easier and more profitable targets. Yet the thief had left the public exhibition halls untouched, stolen the bronze ding, and even left behind a more "valuable" protruding-eye figure. Clearly money was not the motive. Whoever did this knew the ding's special function. That meant the case had to be approached through food.

Lin Fei did not go home. Instead he turned into an internet cafe, logged into the Douban group *Chengdu's Culinary Utopia*, and began scanning for recent restaurant gossip. The first thing he saw was a fresh post:

*San Zhi Er's Heirloom Hotpot Stolen, Suspected Prank by Rich Kids (repost)*

One of Chengdu's most famous hotpot brands, San Zhi Er, suffered a theft last night. The treasure of its flagship branch on Nijiaqiao Road, a three-handled bronze hotpot pot said to date back to the Qianlong era, mysteriously disappeared. The interior of the pot was engraved with the house's secret hotpot base recipe, and it still contained the seasoned broth that had been maintained for more than a century. According to the restaurant, the strangest thing was that although the thieves took the heirloom pot, they left behind a gold pot of exactly the same size. The owners suspect that the incident may have been staged as a prank by some local rich second-generation youths...

The post was unverified and came from a burner account, but Lin Fei's eyes lit up at once. The method was almost identical to the Sanxingdui theft. Very likely the same culprit. Nijiaqiao Road was right near Yulin, and Lin Fei reached it quickly.

The flagship branch of San Zhi Er occupied the first floor of the San Zhi Er Building on Nijiaqiao Road, lavishly decorated and a world away from the humble roadside hotpot place it had once been. Lin Fei could not help sighing. Chengdu people love food, but what they love most are inconspicuous little shops hidden in lanes and alleys. Chengdu dialect even has a special term for them: *fly restaurants* — shabby-looking places that could never pass for refined dining, yet offer the truest flavors.

When he entered San Zhi Er, the dinner rush was already over, yet the main hall was still packed and roaring with noise. He waited a long time before a small table against the wall opened up. He casually ordered a beef-tallow broth and a few common dishes — duck tongues, goose intestine, bean curd skin, thousand-layer tripe, crisp sausage — then settled in to eat while studying the hall with deliberate care.

At first glance everything looked ordinary, just another busy evening in a popular hotpot restaurant. But on closer inspection, small irregularities emerged. Yellow police tape was tangled among the flower stands behind the main door. The wall by the dish window still bore faint chalk marks. The upstairs private seating area was closed, and several black-clad guards who usually parked cars outside now stood at the foot of the staircase. So the Douban post had been true: a criminal case really had happened here last night, and the police had really come. The question now was what, exactly, had taken place.

Lin Fei took from his backpack a camera no larger than a palm, with a shoulder strap and leather case. The silver lens was ringed with dense markings and numbers, and across the black front cap were several lines of Cyrillic: *Smena 8M*. It was a 135 camera made in the Soviet Union in 1970, a recognized member of the LOMO family.

LOMO is short for *Leningradskoje Optiko Mechanitscheskoje Objedinenie*. Originally it referred to the LC-A 35mm automatic-exposure rangefinder camera produced by the Leningrad Optical-Mechanical Association, the Soviet Union's largest optical-equipment factory, back in the 1950s. In 1991, two art students from Vienna traveled to Prague, bought a discontinued LC-A on a whim, and were astonished by the photographs it produced. Its lens geometry allowed it to shoot day and night without flash, and it was peculiarly sensitive to red, blue, and yellow, yielding dazzling colors. Before long LOMO had become a global cult among young people, darling of underground art and cultural circles alike.

Lin Fei was a committed LOMO enthusiast himself, but his Smena 8M was no ordinary camera. The 8M's defining trait was that advancing the film was not mechanically coupled to the shutter. That meant the same frame could be exposed again and again, producing images of extraordinary strangeness. Lin Fei took out a marker, scribbled several illegible talismanic symbols onto a note card, and stuck it to the back of the camera. Then, under cover of a trip to the restroom, he wandered around the hall of San Zhi Er snapping away through an entire roll of film. He followed that up with close-up digital shots of every corner using a Ricoh GRD II, and only then returned to his table and finished off the hotpot like a man clearing battlefield debris. Chengdu hotpot has its own character: numbing without brutality, hot without dryness, deeply fragrant, and lingering long on the palate. Lin Fei burped one long, lingering burp of appreciation and finally went home at his usual leisurely pace.
