> LOMO is part of life. — The LOMO Rules

Lin Fei's cramped apartment was not far from Jiuyan Bridge, tucked behind the notorious half-finished building east of it. It was only a one-bedroom flat, but he had wrapped the living room in thick black curtains and turned it into a darkroom.

The moment he got home, he plunged into that darkroom and developed the roll he had just shot. *Feilin* was the old Chinese rendering of *film*, now mostly used for photographic film or printing negatives. Lin Fei had fallen in love with photography in part because of that word alone: it was almost a perfect gloss on his own name. Lin Fei possessed a pair of eyes that the Ancient Shu called *demon-subduing nightmare pupils*, eyes capable of seeing events that had taken place in times invisible to ordinary people. Combined with his LOMO cameras, that gift allowed him to take photographs of unbelievable things.

What he had photographed at San Zhi Er was the scene from the previous night. The Smena 8M's ability to expose the same frame over and over meant that a span of time could be compressed into one strip of film. Sure enough, among the thirty-six developed photographs, two contained a strange dark figure.

They showed the restaurant at one in the morning, just after closing time. Though the hall was dim, the ISO-1600 film still captured a stout shadow moving from corner to corner. The figure knocked over a row of jars of infused liquor at the bar and rummaged through the refrigerators as if looking for something. In the last few overlapping exposures of the second photograph, the figure could be seen descending from upstairs with a huge round object on its back and striding away.

That, presumably, was San Zhi Er's heirloom.

Resting his chin on one hand, Lin Fei stared at the broad back for a long moment. A sensation of familiarity rose within him. I have definitely seen this thing before, he thought. Or rather: what kind of thing is it? After pondering a while, he imported the close-up digital shots from the GRD II into his computer and began examining them one by one. The GRD II, beloved as a secondary camera by professional photographers, had an excellent fixed lens. Lin Fei had fitted it with an infrared filter marked with ritual seals, enabling it to capture certain special images.

He focused on the bar and the refrigerators. Magnified one thousand times, the surfaces showed wavering, rippled claw marks.

"Rippled?" he muttered. "Could it be..."

He immediately logged into the Ancient Shu internal archive on Dingding Cat and pulled up images of the stolen bronze ding from Sanxingdui. Then he searched online for photographs of San Zhi Er's missing heirloom pot. Side by side, the answer became obvious. The three-handled copper pot from San Zhi Er was almost identical in shape to the lost bronze ding, right down to the *taotie* motif worked across its surface. Plainly it was a later imitation of the ancient vessel. But why would anyone steal two such similar cauldrons?

At the lower right corner of the desktop, a Gtalk chat window started blinking. Mendenger's avatar flashed.

Mendenger: I just got word that San Zhi Er's heirloom has been stolen too.

Dingcat: I just got back from eating there.

Mendenger: Ah, then did you...

Dingcat: Of course I know. And I discovered that their heirloom pot is nearly identical to the stolen bronze ding. Don't tell me you're hiding something.

Mendenger: What could I hide? But I do need to tell you this: Sanxingdui's three-handled bronze ding originally came as a pair. The other one was accidentally smashed while being moved to the new museum. The designs on the two vessels matched each other and together formed a set of ancient pictographs. Rumor says they concealed a Sichuan recipe book.

Dingcat: That's absurdly science-fictional. A recipe book? The Elder told me our Ancient Shu ancestors only knew how to boil fish. And chilies didn't even come to China until a few hundred years ago. What Sichuan cuisine could there have been?

Mendenger: Believe it or not. The point is this: over the last century or so, several master chefs in Chengdu — all of them of Shu blood — were allowed to study the bronze ding. Afterward they made imitations of it with slight variations but the same patterns: pots, jars, kettles, all kinds of cookware. Those objects are now scattered across Chengdu, many of them becoming treasured house pieces in restaurants. San Zhi Er's copper pot is one of them.

Dingcat: Got it.

Mendenger: Got what?

Dingcat: The thief is trying to collect every vessel modeled on the bronze ding so he can reconstruct the patterns from the smashed one. There must have been imitations based on that broken vessel too. He's trying to piece together that legendary recipe book, if it really exists.

Mendenger: That makes sense. Clear logic. Sound deduction.

Dingcat: Do you know how many copies there are altogether?

Mendenger: Do you know Chengdu's old nickname?

Dingcat: What? Don't play games.

Mendenger: Turtle City. Corresponding to Chengdu's eight corners.

Dingcat: Damn. I'm logging off.

Lin Fei closed Gtalk and studied the blurry fat silhouette again. Now that every clue was winding tighter and tighter around food, the thief had to be a glutton of the first order.

"A glutton?" he murmured. Then another thought flashed. He looked again at the rippling claw marks. "Wait. Aren't those *taotie* patterns?"

The *taotie* is a mythical beast of ancient China, most commonly seen today only as a decorative motif on bronzes. After the Western Zhou, the design vanished abruptly from Chinese ornament, and no one knows why. In truth, the disappearance had everything to do with the Zhou turning on and destroying the Shu armies that had helped them. Later rulers preferred not to acknowledge that history, and so the emblem of Shu culture vanished from the historical record. The taotie itself became one of the most obscure beasts in Chinese lore and had all manner of ugly reputations hung upon it. Its most famous characteristic was greed for food. Old texts call it monstrous, hairy, fierce, and insatiably gluttonous. In fact, like the Ancient Shu, the taotie had never vanished at all. It had simply remained quietly in the land of Ba and Shu. Lin Fei had seen the great creatures more than once. They were nothing like the terrifying monsters of legend. If anything, they were gentle and unexpectedly endearing. The problem was that they were a secretive bunch and did not care much for the internet. Finding one in a hurry would not be easy.

So where would the ding-stealing taotie go next? Lin Fei rolled his eyes once, and a plan came to him. If there were eight imitations, then he would search them by direction. The gates of old Chengdu had long since burned away in history, but Chengdu people still divided the city into east gate, south gate, west gate, and north gate. Yulin lay at the south gate. So east gate would have to be next.

> Shoot from the hip. — The LOMO Rules

Sichuan people love heat, but they do not call chili peppers *lajiao*. They call them *haijiao*, "sea peppers," for a simple reason: chilies reached China from Central and South America by sea. In Chengdu there is even a street called Haijiao City Street, once the great inland market of the southwest for chili trading, pungent with spice from morning to night. Between Haijiao and the Lianhua neighborhood runs a hidden little lane that comes alive at sunset, lined from one end to the other with small food stalls. Those in the know call it simply the snack street. Lin Fei preferred to call it Back Street. The reason was just as simple: it sat right behind his own place.

It was nearly midnight when Lin Fei parked in Lianhua First District, wound his way into Back Street, and heard the noise before he saw the crowd. In Chengdu, wherever the food is good, the people are never lacking. The whole lane was jammed with tables. Half-drunk men sat bare-chested, shouting across the night, while women leaned in close together and sipped VE soy milk in small careful mouthfuls. That, too, was a kind of local magic. In a fully commercial city, among countless possible drinks, women in these food streets all preferred one specific brand of peanut soy beverage. Decades earlier, the Ancient Shu who guarded Chengdu had quietly laced it with a particular spice, producing an instinctive dependence. Revenue from that drink remained one of the most stable income streams of the Ancient Shu Relief Fund.

Lin Fei had intended to walk the length of Back Street snapping pictures whenever the mood took him, searching for clues as he went. But no sooner had he entered than the owner of his favorite taro chicken dry-pot stall recognized him and physically dragged him in. The aroma wafting from the diners' pots made resistance impossible. Fine, Lin Fei thought. He could begin the investigation over a proper meal. Besides, nobody should question his appetite. In Chengdu, even a delicate young woman dedicated to slimming down can easily make a night of hopping between several eateries.

As he ate, his eyes never stopped moving through the crowd. Kitchens were in the back rooms of each shop, off-limits by the old rules, so all he could do was search from outside. His intuition told him the thief was here somewhere. The light was erratic, the street choked with bodies. Lin Fei took off his T-shirt and draped it over the chair back, concealing beneath it an LC-A, the most classic of LOMO cameras. A cable release ran from the hot shoe into his hand. He set the shutter to 1/500 and loaded ultra-fast film. That combination could capture things too quick for the eye to notice.

Ice jelly! White fungus soup! Lotus-seed sweet soup! Guiling jelly!

Green olives! Green olives!

Shoe shine! Shoe shine!

In Chengdu, every place where people gather to eat also attracts itinerant sellers. Many hyperlocal specialties and services — ice jelly, ear-cleaning, all sorts of minor trades too marginal to sustain a shop of their own — survive only through these wandering vendors. They usually have agreements with the restaurants and can walk in and out freely to conduct their own little business.

Lin Fei had eaten too much already and wanted nothing more. But just as he lowered his head to take a sip of tea, one of the backpack straps hanging over the chair slid down of its own accord, as if someone had brushed past it. He looked up. No one had passed. He thought for a moment, then at once pulled out an Agat-18K and began snapping in the direction before him.

The 18K was a classic half-frame camera. Though it used normal 135 film, each exposure occupied only half a frame, meaning that a roll of thirty-six could produce seventy-two images. It had once even been used as a spy camera and produced exceptionally sharp results, perfect for quick shots. There was no time to find a place to develop the roll. Lin Fei simply threw his T-shirt over his head, opened the camera, and used his nightmare pupils to read the negatives directly. His brain was strong enough to reverse the images in his head, but staring directly at undeveloped film strained the eyes terribly. After only a few frames he felt light-headed.

Still, the effort paid off. One series of half-frame negatives showed with perfect clarity that only a few meters away a hunched figure was pushing an ice-jelly cart down the lane. The edges of the figure were blurred, as though it were moving at a speed no human eye could properly catch.

Lin Fei leaped from his chair, sprinted forward a few paces, and slapped hard at a patch of air in the center of the lane. His palm landed on a cotton overcoat. Slowly, an emaciated old peddler and his battered four-wheeled cart emerged into view.

The old man turned in surprise. Half the turtle-jelly he was holding splashed away. Grinning, Lin Fei said, "Qu Shan'er, what the devil are you doing selling ice jelly out here? And why were you sneaking away from me when you saw me?"

The man was surnamed Qu. In his day he had been the famous Pushcart Qu. In Chengdu dialect, *qushan'er* means earthworm, and because the old man was hunchbacked the nickname had attached itself to him. With a sheepish grin he replied, "So it's Lin-wa after all. Didn't notice you just now. Sorry, sorry."

"Come off it." Lin Fei dipped himself a brimming bowl of cold jelly and, despite the pained twitch in Qu's face, heaped raisins onto it. Eating as he spoke, he said, "Your eyes are too sharp to miss me. Were you just stingy about your snacks again?"

Qu Shan'er forced a smile. "If I greet you, I can't charge you. And one sitting from you can empty half my cart. Then my whole night's work is for nothing." Pushcart Qu had long since become one of those invisible vendors who sold cheaply only to women, children, and beggars. His cart was cloaked in illusion and his legs fitted with talismans that let him range across all of Chengdu every night. Ordinary people never saw him at all. Running into him on Back Street tonight was pure luck.

"All right, all right, I won't eat you out of house and home every time. Tell me something. Do you know any place around here that uses a three-handled bronze pot? About this big." He gestured with his hands. "Same sort of size as that big dragon-spout tea kettle on your cart. With those same patterns on it." His gesture reached the kettle, and he froze.

Nearly every ice-jelly cart in Chengdu carried a large dragon-spout copper kettle, but Qu Shan'er's was different. Besides the handle, it had an ear on each side, making three in all, and the patterns worked across its body were unmistakably *taotie* motifs.

"Hold on," Lin Fei said, his mouth falling open. "Qu Shan'er, how did I never notice this? Your kettle is shaped exactly like Sanxingdui's three-handled ding."

"Heh." Qu stroked it fondly. "Family heirloom. Been in use nearly a hundred years."

Lin Fei ran a hand along the smooth body of the kettle and thought triumphantly: No need to search the whole city after all. If the thief wants it, he'll come here. I'll just follow Qu Shan'er and wait like a hunter by the tree.

At that moment, a child came lurching over, holding up a coin. "Uncle," he said, "I want some ice jelly."

"Coming right up!" Qu pushed Lin Fei aside and deftly began preparing a bowl. The boy's fringe hung over his forehead in an almost adorable line, and Lin Fei crouched to pat him on the head. "What's your name, kid?"

The child's expression changed instantly. In a cold voice he said, "My name is Tao Tao. My dad said you'd definitely touch my forehead. Once you did, you'd be done for, and then he could take the big kettle."

Lin Fei spun around in shock. Qu Shan'er now stood empty-handed, and the dragon-spout kettle had vanished, replaced by a brand-new gleaming golden one. Far ahead, a dark shape skimmed faintly above the crowd. Lin Fei turned back to seize the boy — but grabbed only empty air. The child had vanished too.

"All right then," Lin Fei said through his teeth. "You want to play it that way?" He turned to Qu Shan'er. "I'll get it back for you." Then he vaulted onto a scooter at the roadside and shot off in pursuit of the fading black figure, leaving behind a stunned old vendor and his cart.
